By the time you land for U.S. grad school, the big tasks (visa, housing, accounts) are already done. What comes next is the everyday admin that makes daily life actually work, and getting the order wrong makes each step block the next. No phone makes opening a bank account a hassle; no bank means no card; no SSN delays your license and your credit. So this post walks through the University of Florida (UF) settlement sequence exactly as it runs, from phone to bank to SSN to Florida driver's license to building credit, and spells out what to bring and what to watch at each stage.

> Recommended order: phone → bank → I-94/check-in → SSN → license → credit card. Each earlier step is often a prerequisite for the next, so keeping the sequence makes everything smoother.

## 1. U.S. Phone (Arrival Day)

Get a U.S. number first thing after you land. Bank setup, SSN applications, and every signup form will ask for one. The good news: this is the easiest step.

- Prepaid / MVNO plans require no SSN and no credit check. With a passport + address + card, you can activate an eSIM instantly, right at the airport.

| Carrier | Network | Approx. monthly |
|---|---|---|
| Mint Mobile | T-Mobile | ~$15 if paid annually (best value) |
| US Mobile | Choice of 3 networks | Unlimited ~$16.6 |
| Visible (Verizon) | Verizon | $25 unlimited |
| Major carrier prepaid | Own network | $30~$50 |

- Postpaid plans require an SSN or a deposit. Start prepaid early on; you can move to postpaid once you have an SSN and some credit; there's no rush.
- Keep your Korean number alive via "number hold (suspension)" or a dual-SIM setup. You'll still need it for bank and KakaoTalk OTP verification.

## 2. Bank Account (Possible Without an SSN)

Once you have a U.S. number, the bank is next. Students can open an account even without an SSN yet, so there's no reason to wait on the SSN first.

- An in-branch visit is required. International students often can't open online. Bring: passport + I-20 + (if available) I-94 + proof of U.S. address + phone number.
- Common options: Chase College Checking ($0/month for students, many branches near campus), BoA, Wells Fargo. New-account bonuses of $100~$300 are a frequent promotion, so check for them.
- The selection criterion is simple: pick by branch and ATM density near campus, which is what makes daily life easiest.

## 3. SSN (Social Security Number)

As a GA you receive a salary, so you're eligible for an SSN. You just can't apply the moment you arrive; the process requires waiting a few days.

- No applying right after entry. Only after SEVIS is activated (your ISSS check-in is processed, typically about 10 days after arrival) and from 30 days before your employment start date.
- Steps:
  1. Complete ISSS check-in → receive your SSN support letter (requires a handwritten signature from both your advisor and employer).
  2. Apply online at [ssa.gov](https://www.ssa.gov/number-card).
  3. Visit your local SSA office (by appointment).
  4. The card arrives by mail in about 2~4 weeks.
- Bring: passport · I-94 · I-20 · SSN support letter · non-citizen questionnaire · offer letter showing EIN, position, work dates, and supervisor.

## 4. Driver's License: Korea-Florida Reciprocity (Tests Waived)

This is the step Korean students especially welcome. Florida recognizes Korean licenses reciprocally, so you skip almost all the testing.

- Florida designates the Republic of Korea as a license-reciprocity country → written and road tests are waived; only a vision test is required. (Reciprocity countries: Canada, France, Germany, Taiwan, Korea.)
- Where: a county Tax Collector office, appointment required. (In Gainesville: [alachuacollector.com](https://www.alachuacollector.com/nonimmigrants/))
- Bring: passport · F-1 visa · I-94 · I-20 · Korean license · two proofs of residency · SSN or proof you can't be issued one. Cost is about $48, and it's issued as a Real ID.
- IDP (International Driving Permit): get one before you leave Korea (you must carry the physical Korean license with it). Use it for temporary driving and rentals until you exchange for the Florida license.

> Confirm that reciprocity is still current via the [FLHSMV new-resident](https://www.flhsmv.gov/new-resident/) page and the Tax Collector before you go.

## 5. Building Credit From Zero

The last step takes time, so the earlier you start the better. In the U.S., your credit score quietly governs a lot.

- A U.S. credit score affects apartments, cars, and postpaid phone plans. As a foreign newcomer, you start from zero.
- Before an SSN: a secured card (your deposit is your limit) or a student card for international students (Deserve EDU, Zolve, Firstcard, etc., available with an ITIN or no SSN).
- After an SSN: move up to a Discover it Student or Capital One student card.
- The key habit is just one: pay in full every month and keep your utilization low, and credit typically builds within 6~12 months.

Finish these five steps in order and daily life in the U.S. starts to run on its own. Figures like visa, tax, and license rules are current as of writing and can change, so re-check the official sites right before you act. More study-abroad and settlement resources continue at seunghoonchoi.com.
