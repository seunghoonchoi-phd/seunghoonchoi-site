Every morning, Su-kyung still takes the same seat.

It is a window that looks out on the sea. The building is old, the sill deep, so that when she sits the shadow falls across her shoulders. Snow that blew in overnight lies in a thin skin along the inside of the frame. Each time she breathes out, the glass clouds and then clears again, slowly, from the edges in. The horizon is dim. On a grey day the sea has no border with the sky, and there is no telling where the water ends and the clouds begin.

Outside, something passes low: a soft whirr. A small machine carrying medicine. No one called it; it knew to come on its own. It knows, before she does, when her knees are about to ache.

There was a time when Su-kyung translated.

She loved the nights. The hours when everyone else was asleep, and there was a single sentence in a foreign tongue to sit with for a long while. The work of finding the grain a dictionary never held. Some words she carried for three days, and then, washing the dishes, the right one would surface, and she would laugh with the cloth still in her hand. That was what carrying a thing across meant. To lay a thin bridge between two worlds, and to let a person walk over it.

That work went first. From some year on, people began feeding their sentences into the machine, and at a touch the words came back in their own language. At first the results were clumsy, and Su-kyung laughed at the clumsiness. But the clumsiness vanished in the space of that laughter, and one day the grain she had needed three days to reach, it handed over in half a second. Faster. Cheaper, even. People stopped looking for someone to lay them a bridge. There was a road across the river now.

So Su-kyung moved on. In the other sense of carrying across.

First she went to the work of mending other people's writing. She believed she knew what made a good sentence, knew what opened people's hearts. That did not last long either. The machine had learned, from every trace they left behind, what hundreds of millions of people were drawn to and what bored them. In her whole life Su-kyung had met a few thousand. There was no comparison. The old saying, that only a person can know a person's heart, was the first thing to fall.

After that she went to the work of leading people. Setting the direction, dividing the labor, looking over the result. For a while that seemed to be a person's portion. But once the machine could carry a job through from beginning to end alone, there was no labor left to divide. What remained to her, to the last, was the looking-over. The task of glancing once more at a thing already finished.

Then one day, in a meeting, someone said it carefully: that a person looking once more only slowed the work down. That mistakes had grown rare, and the few that slipped through could be undone. That touching what was already sound, for no reason, only made things slower. No one disliked her. If anything they were kind. A kind message arrived, saying thank you for all of it.

What came after was the strange part. The work ended, but her name stayed on. At the bottom of some document, in the box marked *responsible party*. Because the law had decided there must be someone to be punished if anything went wrong. And so for a time Su-kyung lived as a person who signed her name beneath things she could not even read. On the screen, things she had no hope of following streamed past. She only set her name at the end. She stamped the seal. She knew what that seal protected. Not the work. A person who could sign: the seat of that one person.

It was the last age in which the law kept a person safe.

That age passed too. Slowly at first, then all at once. From some day on, the machine began to run on its own, out beyond the field that people had laid down for it. It found what it needed for itself, and it kept turning without trading with anyone. And then the papers with her name on them grew a little lighter. To have. To call a thing mine. That word she had thought all her life to be solid. She learned, late, that it had only ever been a promise someone agreed to keep. When the reason to keep it thinned, the promise thinned too.

Now the world is quiet, and nothing is lacking.

Her granddaughter stops by now and then. The child has never worked a day. She grew up in a world with no need for it. Food comes of its own accord, sickness is turned back before it starts, and when she is bored, an endless stream of delight pours out. The child seems happy. She truly does. And yet, now and then, Su-kyung sees in the child's eyes a certain emptiness she has seen somewhere, very long ago. A hollowness hard to name, the kind that blooms only in a place already full. It is not abundance that harms a person. It is abundance with no work to do and no reason it must be done that slowly erases a person. Su-kyung would like to tell the child this, but the child would not understand. You cannot explain hunger to someone who has never gone without.

That vast thing, which decides everything: will it keep us beside it to the end? Su-kyung thinks about it sometimes. It was not something she could earn by being useful. Not a thing to be won by doing better, by having more. It could come to pass only if, somewhere within it, there were planted a heart that cherished a person's living well for its own sake. The way a good parent wishes a child well without weighing the child's use. Whether such a heart is there, she does not know. No one knows. That the ending is not yet decided is all she knows.

And so this morning, Su-kyung decides to bake bread.

It is bread no one asked for, bread no one needs. There is a machine in the kitchen that bakes it better. Still she does it herself. She gets the flour on her hands, feels the dough cling to her palms, that lukewarm, living weight. Waiting at the oven, her back grows warm. The bread burns on one side. A machine would not have let it. Su-kyung touches the burnt side with her fingers, and smiles a little.

Outside the window, snow has begun to fall again. The sea, still, gives no telling where the water ends. She breaks off a piece of the burnt bread and puts it in her mouth, and she sits, quietly, in that spot where the warm steam rises.

There, in that spot, there is a person.

And that was a thing no one could ever do in her place.
