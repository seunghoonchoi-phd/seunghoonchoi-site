When an electrode is attached to skin or a plant leaf, the surface creates problems quickly. Sweat, water, curvature, rough texture, and motion disturb contact between the electrode and the surface. Stable signal recording needs both material design and structural design.

This page gathers my bio-inspired electrode, sensor, and soft robotics research in one place. It shows how octopus-like suction structures, hydrogel interfaces, conductive fibers, and soft gripper designs connect to signal recording and robotic control.

## Core Idea

Bio-inspired research does not copy nature as decoration. It studies a structure such as an octopus suction cup and redesigns it for real electrode and sensor problems: water, motion, roughness, and unstable contact.

For EMG electrodes, contact between skin and electrode matters. The signal has to remain stable even when skin is wet or moving, especially for wearable robots and prosthetic control.

For plant electrodes, the leaf surface is a harder target. Leaves are rough, moisture changes, and damage matters. The interface has to be gentle while still recording signals reliably.

For soft grippers, structure, sensors, and control have to work together. A soft robotic hand needs a gripping structure, a way to sense contact, and a control strategy that turns signals into action.

## Questions to Ask

- What surface does the electrode have to read signals from.
- How do water, sweat, motion, and roughness disturb contact.
- How should an idea from nature change when it enters a real material and process.
- How does the sensor signal connect to robotic control or biosignal analysis.
- In which environment should the result be tested again.

These questions point to one center of the work: making materials and structures that do not lose signals on difficult real surfaces.
