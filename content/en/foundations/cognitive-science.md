AI was built by modeling human intelligence, and in the end it gets used on people. So once you know how people see, remember, and judge, and where they go wrong **in regular ways**, you start to see how AI can help people and how it can push them around.

The most useful lesson cognitive science offers is this: human mistakes aren't random — they **lean in consistent directions**. Once you know that lean, you look again at both your own judgment and the screens other people build.

## What it covers

- **Perception** — You look at how the senses become information. We don't see the world as it is; we see a picture the brain has filtered and built.
- **Memory** — You look at what stays and what gets changed or lost. Memory isn't a recording; it's rebuilt each time, so even things that really happened quietly change.
- **Language** — It's the structure for trading thoughts through words. The same sentence lands differently depending on who says it and how.
- **Learning** — You look at how experience turns into ability. The way people learn is a long-standing hint for how to teach AI.
- **Judgment** — It's how you decide in uncertain situations. To save time we lean on rough guesses (heuristics), which are mostly right but sometimes miss badly.
- **Illusion** — It's the habit of people seeing things wrong in consistent directions. Biases like overtrusting what you saw and picking out only what you wanted to see are in everyone.

## Why it matters more in the AI era

The point where AI helps people, and the point where it sways them, both hang on these gaps in cognition. Why a recommendation feed is so hard to put down, why we so easily believe AI-made fakes — it's all explained here. Once you know the weak spots in your own judgment, you can design AI better and use it with less risk of being pushed around.

## Where it leads

Cognitive science moves [Philosophy](../philosophy/)'s "knowing" question into experiments, and the AI of [Computer Science](../computer-science/) borrowed many of its ideas from these models of cognition. Knowing how people go wrong is also used in [Economics](../economics/) to understand the choices people make.
