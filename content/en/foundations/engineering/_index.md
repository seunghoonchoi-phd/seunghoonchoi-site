There's a longer distance than you'd think between a good idea and a thing people actually use every day. Engineering is the work of closing that distance. For an AI model too, going past a flashy demo to become **a product that runs every day** means it has to get through every one of these stages.

The core of engineering isn't "making it work once" — it's "making it work every time." Something that succeeded once by luck and something that works the same whether it rains or the crowds pour in are problems on entirely different levels.

## Why it matters more in the AI era

Thanks to AI, building a demo has become astonishingly easy. So the contest has moved past the demo. The real difference shows up in the cost of verifying, rebuilding, and operating without stopping. It's common for something that looked great on screen to give it all back in those costs once you actually run it.

## Where it leads

Within the limits [Physics](../physics/) has drawn, engineering turns the results of [Computer Science](../computer-science/) into real things. Questions of safety and responsibility meet [Law](../law/), and questions of cost and scale meet [Economics](../economics/).
