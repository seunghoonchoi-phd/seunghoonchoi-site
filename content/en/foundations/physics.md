No matter how plausible an answer AI gives, reality never breaks the laws of nature. Once you know how much energy something takes and what is physically impossible, you can tell **a simulation on screen apart from what actually works**.

Physics isn't a subject where you memorize formulas. It's reading the rules the world always keeps and drawing the line that says "this far works, beyond it doesn't." With that line, you stop wasting time on plans that can't happen.

## What it covers

- **Laws of nature** — These are the rules reality follows without exception. Even a good idea won't work in the real world if it breaks these rules.
- **Matter** — It's the materials the world is made of and how they behave. The same design works or fails depending on which material you build it from.
- **Energy** — It's how much it costs to do something. Even training a giant AI model is, in the end, a physical event that burns enormous amounts of electricity.
- **Measurement** — It's how you measure and compare and turn that into fact. One well-taken number beats a hundred claims.
- **Real-world limits** — It's the boundary of what can't be done in principle. Knowing what's impossible lets you pour your resources onto what's possible.

## Why it matters more in the AI era

AI easily churns out plausible numbers, but whether they match experiments and the field is a different matter. Catching the point where the two diverge has, in the end, always come down to a person with physical intuition. On top of that, the power, heat, and material limits of the data centers that hold up AI are themselves physics problems, so people who know this field are needed more and more.

## Where it leads

Physics uses [Mathematics](../math/) as the language of its laws, and it draws the line for [Engineering](../engineering/) that says "this far is possible." The data you get from measurement leads on into [Computer Science](../computer-science/).
