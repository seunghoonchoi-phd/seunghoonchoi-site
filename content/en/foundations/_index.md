Put AI to work and the answers come fast. The hard part is telling whether an answer is right and where it went wrong. In the end, that judgment falls to a person who has the fundamentals.

The eight tracks gathered here are those fundamentals. The concept map below shows how the eight fields connect to one another, and the four groups beneath it lay them out in order of priority. Click into any one and the concepts that field covers will open up (for now I've only sketched the placeholders, and I'm filling them in one by one).

## Why these eight

I chose them so that they don't overlap (each asks a different question) yet leave no big gap when combined (together they cover form, nature, mind, and society).

- **Form & Computation** (mathematics, computer science): What is true, and what can be computed.
- **Nature & Making** (physics, engineering): What rules reality follows, and what can be built.
- **Mind & Meaning** (cognitive science, philosophy): How people come to know, and what is right and meaningful.
- **Society & Institutions** (economics, law): How limited resources are divided, and what is allowed.

The fields borrow from one another: computer science borrows from mathematics, engineering from physics, and so on. But the questions they ask don't overlap, so no one of them can stand in for another. The lines in the concept map below are exactly those borrowing relationships, and AI sits right where computer science and cognitive science meet.
