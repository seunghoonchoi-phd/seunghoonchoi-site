You solved problems like this in middle school. x + 2 = 8. One answer: 6. It's 6 no matter who solves it, and once you've learned it, every future instance takes five seconds. It's knowledge whose copying cost rounds down to zero. The world is full of this kind of knowledge. You can search for it, write it in a single line, and the moment it's written, anyone can repeat it.

But take the same kind of problem and mix in two or three variables, and the landscape changes. If you don't know x, don't know y, and the right value of y depends on how you set x, then the answer is no longer a single thing. It becomes a cluster of answers that shift with conditions. Real know-how mostly lives in this second world. Yet we keep trying to write know-how in the language of the first world — as 'the one right answer.' That's why it gets copied, and why it gets copied even faster in the age of AI.

## The recipe travels; the cook's feel doesn't

Think about cooking. A famous restaurant's full recipe leaks online more often than you'd think — grams of each ingredient, heat level, timing, all of it. And yet you can follow it exactly and the dish still doesn't taste like theirs. Strange. You have all the information, but not the result.

The reason is simple. The written recipe is just a single point measured under average conditions. In the actual kitchen, the cook looks at how watery today's ingredients came in and pulls back a spoonful of liquid; feels with the back of the hand that the pan is hotter than usual and cuts the heat thirty seconds early; tastes not for 'slightly bland' but for 'the kind of bland that turns salty once it cools.' None of this makes it onto paper. Not because it can't be written, but because it was never a single value to begin with — it's a judgment that moves with the situation.

If the recipe is 'one answer,' the cook's feel is 'a map of different answers for different conditions.' The line between what gets copied and what doesn't sits exactly there.

## When variables interlock, the answer space explodes

Let's sharpen it. One variable, one answer. Two variables that are independent of each other? Still manageable — you tune each one separately. It gets genuinely hard when the variables interlock: when the right value of A depends on how you set B, and the right value of B depends in turn on C.

Once that interlocking appears, the very notion of 'the answer' collapses. What remains is conditional answers. 'Set the temperature like this, then the time like that — but only when the ingredient is in this state.' The same settings succeed one day and fail the next. Not because the settings were wrong, but because the state of the other variables they quietly assumed had changed.

Go, the board game, makes this vivid. A handful of standard sequences are printed in every book. But when to use one — at what point in the whole-board situation it becomes a good move and when it becomes a blunder — lives only in the body of someone who has played tens of thousands of games. AI beat the best humans not by memorizing those sequences but by playing itself far more times than any person could, mapping the interlocking space more densely. It learned the conditions between the moves, not the moves.

## The real asset is the failure map

But the heart of know-how isn't the settings that work. The more valuable thing is knowledge of how it fails. I call this the failure map.

Three things are written on the failure map. First, what breaks the result — which variable, if you touch it, sends everything off the rails at once. Second, how order binds it — the points where doing the same task with the same materials in a different sequence changes the outcome, the way letting the dough rest first versus last decides the bread. Third, and this is the scariest one: the things that look fine now and die later. Perfect when fresh, cracked three days on; running smoothly in the demo, collapsing in the real environment.

This failure map never makes it into the finished product, the published paper, or the released recipe. Of course not. The finished product shows only the one point that succeeded. The hundreds of failures it took to reach that point, and the boundary line those failures drew — 'do not go in here' — survive nowhere in the output. Yet the thing that saves you time the next time you build something similar isn't the one successful point. It's that boundary line.

## Same motion, different reproduction

There's an interesting border: motions anyone can describe but few can reproduce.

Watch a potter's wheel. Center the clay, keep steady pressure in the hands, match the wheel's speed. Three sentences and you're done. Watch the video a hundred times and your head understands all of it. Then a beginner sits down and the clay flees their hands. The explanation transferred perfectly; the result didn't. The pressure at the fingertips has to respond, moment by moment and conditionally, to the clay's moisture and the spin speed — and that conditional response doesn't transfer through words or video.

The same is true of an instrument, a single movement in sport, the feel for when to go silent at a negotiating table. Describability and reproducibility are different axes. Descriptions copy easily; reproduction does not. And what the copier actually wants to walk away with is always the latter.

## A rough formula for the strength of a secret

To make this visible, you can roughly estimate it like so. How hard a piece of know-how is to copy is roughly proportional to (the number of interlocking variables × knowledge of failure × the cost to recreate it) divided by (how easily it can be accessed × how reverse-engineerable the output is).

Look at the numerator. The more variables and the more they interlock, the more of the failure paths you know, the more time and money it takes to rediscover from scratch — the stronger it is. Look at the denominator. The more the knowledge surfaces with a single search, the more the secret reveals itself when you take the finished product apart — the weaker it is.

So the weakest secret is knowledge that's searchable and fully exposed in the output. x + 2 = 8 is exactly that. The strongest secret is knowledge with many interlocking variables, a long history of failure, and an output that — no matter how hard you take it apart — never shows you which conditions made it work. The cook's feel, the wheel, the sense built over tens of thousands of games: those.

## In the AI era, 'working hard' is not a moat

Back to our own work. Many people believe 'I work hard' is their edge. But if the product of all that hard work is a recipe that's finished the moment it's written down, that's not a moat. AI reads, combines, and reproduces written recipes far faster than any human. A published method belongs to no one the instant it's published.

So what should you accumulate? The answer is clear: knowledge that is expensive to rediscover. Even doing the same work, lean toward burning into your body how that work fails, what must be done in what order, what looks fine on the surface and dies later. Instead of showing off one successful result, hold the map of conditions that lead to it.

That requires changing how you work. Don't only record what went right — record what went wrong. Collect 'this failed because of that' more than 'this is how you do it.' Take a setting that worked once and run it again under changed conditions, and draw with your own hand the boundary of where it holds and where it breaks. That boundary line is precisely the asset no one can restore by examining the finished product alone.

Know-how that resists copying isn't a stroke of genius. It's the thickness of a map drawn by countless failures and a judgment that shifts with every condition. In an age when AI swallows every recipe, the way to remain uncopyable isn't to work harder — it's to accumulate what is expensive to rediscover. To do work that doesn't end the moment it's written down; work that, at the very least, never fully transfers.
