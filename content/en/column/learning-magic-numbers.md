Imagine you're dropped into the middle of an unfamiliar city. No map, no signal on your phone. What's the fastest way to grasp the shape of the place? The answer, surprisingly, is not "walk a lot." Wander down a hundred alleys at random and you'll just get sore legs while your mental picture stays a tangle. The people who orient fastest check only three directions. Once straight ahead, once forty-five degrees to the left, once forty-five degrees to the right. Where do the main roads run? Which side are the tall buildings clustered on? Where's a fixed landmark like a river or a hill? Three glances, and a minimal coordinate system snaps into place: downtown is that way, the edge of town is this way. From then on, every alley you walk lands on a map you already hold in your head.

Entering a new field works the same way. People often ask, "How many cases do I need to see before I get a feel for this?" — as if there were a single magic number. But from dropping in and out of various fields, what I've come to feel is that the answer isn't one number. It's a range that differs by layer. And there's a deeper truth underneath it: what matters in the end is not the count of cases but the angle and the feedback. The same principle as the city — seeing three different directions once each beats walking the same alley a hundred times.

## Three viewpoints: the minimum to get a direction

The first thing you need is exactly three viewpoints, like the three directions in the city.

First: what does good output actually look like? If you're learning to cook, you need to see, at least once, the cross-section of a well-seared steak, the feel of a well-composed photo. Without a target image, you can't even tell whether what you made turned out well or fell apart.

Second: what drives the result? When the same ingredients lead one person to success and another to failure, what's the variable making the difference? For a steak: heat level, cooking time, how long you let the meat rest. In any field, the handful of variables that truly steer the outcome is smaller than you'd expect.

Third: where do beginners fail? Where does a newcomer typically slip — not heating the pan enough, flipping the meat over and over. Knowing other people's failure points in advance lets you skip half your own trial and error.

With these three viewpoints, you have a direction. Not seeing more, but seeing once each from three different angles. That's the start.

## Seven chunks: the dashboard in your head

Once you have a direction, it's time to build a small dashboard in your head. Just as a car's dashboard puts speed, fuel, and engine temperature in one glance, every new field has a set of key gauges you have to watch at once.

But these gauges can't run much past seven. The familiar idea of "seven plus or minus two" — that the number of chunks a person's working memory can hold at one time is roughly five to nine. The mental whiteboard is smaller than you think. If you can't boil the core down to seven or eight chunks and try to juggle twenty at once, the board fills up and there's no room left for new information to land.

Take writing. As a beginner you try to mind spelling, word choice, sentence length, paragraph structure, logical flow, the reader, and the title all at once — and your mind freezes. The skilled writer, by contrast, fuses "spelling and word choice" into a single chunk called "line editing." Several things compress into one, and a hand comes free. With that free hand they grip something larger. The ability to keep the dashboard inside seven slots — that's the doorway to intermediate.

## Twenty to thirty contrasting cases: where patterns surface

Once you have a dashboard, it's time to gather cases in earnest. Here lies a common trap. People usually fixate on "a lot," when what matters is "different kinds."

The point where repeating patterns start to become visible is roughly twenty to thirty cases. But with a decisive condition attached: those cases have to contrast with each other. The success, the failure, the ambiguous in-between, the extreme, and the most common standard case. Only when you lay out cases of such different colors does a rule finally show its outline — "ah, this variable behaves like that, here."

Thirty cases of the same kind, on the other hand, are really just one case seen thirty times. Someone who has looked at thirty photos of perfectly seared steaks has no idea what failure looks like. Memorize thirty easy sentences of the same level in a foreign language and you never meet the moment a sentence collapses or the register shifts. Repetition without contrast only adds volume; it doesn't draw the outline. When gathering cases, don't count the number — count the kinds.

## Seventy to two hundred feedback loops: where the hand steadies

Seeing with the eye and doing with the hand are different problems. Just because the pattern has become visible doesn't mean your hand will follow.

The wobble peculiar to beginners — it worked yesterday but not today, you did the same thing yet got a different result — that instability dies down somewhere around seventy to two hundred repetitions. It's like learning an instrument: you have to play the same passage about a hundred times before your hand moves on its own. A workout's form settling into the body, the nerves draining out of public speaking — they land somewhere in here too.

But one more thing has to be said. These repetitions can't just be a "count"; they have to be a count with feedback attached. Repeat a hundred times without knowing you're wrong and you'll just harden the wrong form a hundred times over. Do it once, check where it went off, fix it, and do it again. Only with this corrective loop do the seventy reps mean anything.

## The real engine is chunking

Behind all these numbers — three, seven, thirty, a hundred — one common engine is actually running. Chunking. The ability to bundle scattered information into a single meaningful unit.

The outline doesn't appear when information gets more plentiful. It appears when information clumps into chunks. A chess master remembers the board at a glance not because of raw memory but because they see twenty pieces as a few chunks — "that's a common attacking formation, that's a defensive shape." Twenty scattered dots to a beginner are three or four meaningful clumps to the master.

So the moment a new field's outline clicks into place is not the moment you cram in more information, but the moment scattered things suddenly bind into a single chunk. "Oh — these were all the same thing." In that instant a slot on your mental dashboard empties, and with the empty slot you grip something larger. The people who learn fast aren't the ones who memorize a lot. They're the ones who bundle often.

## Feedback beats exposure

Finally, one principle that runs through every number. Feedback beats exposure.

Reading something passively a hundred times loses to trying it yourself thirty times with a correction each time. Between someone who only listened to a foreign language for a hundred hours and someone who stumbled through speaking it thirty times, getting "no, you say it like this" every time — anyone who's done it knows who improves faster. Reading and listening are comfortable. So we keep fleeing in that direction. But what carves the outline is the uncomfortable process of cutting into it yourself and checking where it came out wrong.

Exposure piles up the material; feedback carves that material into a shape. Pile the material mountain-high and never carve it once, and you can stare at it for ten years without the outline ever appearing.

## So, a sequence you can use

Tie all of this into one path and it comes out like this.

First, get a direction with **three angles**: what good output looks like, what drives the result, where beginners fail. Next, distill the core into **seven chunks** to build the dashboard in your head. Then gather **thirty contrasting cases** — not by number but by kind: success, failure, ambiguous, extreme, standard. Finally, run **a hundred feedback loops** — not mere repetition, but a do-check-fix loop.

Three → seven → thirty → a hundred. These numbers are not laws. They're just the working breakpoints — the places where, in a practitioner's experience, something tends to shift. Depending on the field they might double or halve. What truly matters isn't the numbers themselves but the two principles underneath them: the outline comes from angle, not volume; skill comes from feedback, not exposure. That story about not walking a hundred alleys in a strange city but checking three directions — it was, all along, a map that applies just the same to every new thing you set out to learn.
