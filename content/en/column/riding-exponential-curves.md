For a while I held onto an idea. The way to beat someone more talented than me is to play a game that grows exponentially while they play one that grows linearly. The intuition is right. But it's only half right.

Talent itself isn't linear either. A talented person who climbs onto compounding machinery — reputation, network, data, intellectual property — goes exponential just the same. So "I'm exponential, they're linear" only holds if the other person foolishly stays linear. A strategy that bets on the other person's irrationality is weak. So I had to change the question. Not how to trap the opponent, but how I should ride so my gains don't leak.

## The same wave, different outcomes

This is where most people get it wrong. The hard part isn't finding the exponential curve — it's how you ride it. The same wave can arrive and your payoff can be exponential or linear depending on how you ride. In order of biggest payoff, there are four ways.

You ride by ownership. You hold a stake in the curve — intellectual property, data, equity. When the curve rises, your share rises with it. You don't work more, and the asset rides the curve. It's the only way the curve's exponential nature feeds straight into the exponential nature of your gain. You ride by complement. As the curve grows, you make what's needed more around it. The bigger AI gets, the more demand grows for verifying and interpreting its output. You're dependent on the curve, but you grow with it. You ride by tool. You use that exponential technology as a multiplier on your own productivity. As the curve improves, your output doubles. The curve pushes you forward — but you own no share.

The last one is the trap. You ride by labor. You just get a job in the field and take part. However high the curve climbs, your share is fixed as a wage. The upside flows to the owners and never reaches you. Bluntly, this is the most common defeat. You reassure yourself with "I got into a promising field, so I'm set," but ride by labor and the curve is exponential while your bank account is linear.

So the question always has to split in two. Is this curve exponential, and am I riding it by ownership or by labor?

## How to tell exponential from linear

The test actually shrinks to one thing. When this grows by one unit, does that growth make the next unit easier, cheaper, and faster? If yes, exponential; if no, linear. The essence of exponential is a self-reinforcing loop. Output becomes the fuel for the next output and accelerates itself.

The signs of exponential look like this. A result draws more resources (capital, data, users), and those resources produce a bigger result. Making one more unit costs steadily less — the more nearly free the copying, as in digital, the better. As users grow, value grows, and that value draws more users. Combining with what already exists makes possibilities multiply.

The signs of linear are the opposite. Output scales with human hands and physical resources — to make more you have to work more. One more unit always costs the same. Doing well doesn't make the next time easier; you start from scratch every time. The market's ceiling is fixed. Whether there's a loop or not — that's the fork.

## Two traps

There are two traps here you absolutely have to tell apart.

One is the S-curve. Every exponential eventually bends into an S-curve. It explodes early, then someday slows toward saturation. So the real question isn't "is it exponential" but "where on the curve am I now." You have to climb on before the inflection point to get the thrust; past the peak, the cost of entry is high and there's no room to rise. Riding a good curve late is a lot like riding a bad one.

The other is fake exponential — a bubble. Capital overheated without a real loop. The test is simple. Does the product actually get better as users grow; does success actually make the next success easier? If money piled in with no self-reinforcing mechanism, it's a bubble. And a bubble is worse than linear. When it bursts, you go negative.

Compressed, it's this. Is the loop real (exponential?), and where on the curve am I right now (timing)? You have to pass both before it's worth climbing on.

## Ride it consciously, or by instinct?

One more thing snagged me here. Life has more than one or two variables, so isn't riding a curve with a sharp goal different from just riding it on instinct?

It is. Riding consciously means grabbing a single variable sharply and pouring resources into it. Directional control and speed are the strengths, but if you picked the wrong variable, that error compounds too. Sharpness is a weapon when it's right and a trap when it's wrong. Riding on instinct means staying scattered across many variables, bumping into the environment until you happen to climb onto a curve. It's slow, uncontrollable, and unrepeatable — but it has the robustness that even if one variable is wrong, it's recovered somewhere else.

The answer isn't one or the other but a combination. Use sharpness only on the core variables, and on the rest deliberately loosen control, leaving yourself exposed to chance, relationships, and unforeseen opportunity. And when something pulls at you on instinct, analyze afterward why it pulls, and recover that as input for the conscious model. Instinct is pattern recognition not yet put into words, not something to ignore. Like a navigator who sets the broad course with the engine and catches the wind with the sail, the choppier the sea of variables, the more you lose by throwing away the sail. Only, if you're going to use sharpness, build in a routine that periodically doubts one thing — is the variable I grabbed still the right one? If the direction is wrong, you'll go faster and farther into the wrong place than someone wandering on instinct.

## In the end, don't go in bare-bodied

An exponential curve is a swell building far out at sea, gathering energy. Linear is the ripple about to break. The test is whether this is a wave that grows itself. But even when the same big wave arrives, the outcome splits. The surfer with a board turns the wave's force into their own thrust and shoots forward; the bare-bodied one is just swept along by the same wave. Its energy never becomes theirs.

I've already seen the good wave (AI, computational science). So what's left is not going in bare-bodied. I go into that wave — but I go in carrying the board of ownership. And a swell gives you thrust only if you climb on just before it breaks — too early and there's no force, after it's broken you're only swept into the foam.

So if something just surfaced that makes you think "this is new — is it exponential?", just ask two things in turn. Is there a self-reinforcing loop here, for real? And am I riding this by ownership, or just going in by labor?
