Point your camera at a menu in a foreign restaurant and it turns into your own language on the spot. Ten years ago you'd have opened a dictionary and fumbled through it one word at a time. At first it feels remarkable, and before long you use it without a second thought. Yet this small scene holds the first button in the order in which AI takes over human work. It starts with the work whose answer is obvious to anyone, the work where you can check right away whether it's right or wrong.

This piece follows those first five stages. The first three are work that gets verified as "this is correct," and the last two are the seat where the work is carried all the way through, and the seat where a person watches over it.

## Where right and wrong are clear is where AI is strongest

First, one thing worth flagging. The work AI is especially strong at has something in common: it's work that gets scored right or wrong on the spot.

In Go you win or you lose. How a protein folds, you find out by matching it against the real structure. Code with tests attached either passes or throws up a red line. In this kind of work, AI tries again and again, fixes its mistakes immediately, and tries again. A person has to sleep, and one slip can sap the will to go on, but a machine, as long as the scoring is clear, runs toward that score through the night. So the clearer the answer, the faster the work falls. The next three stages all belong here.

**Stage 1. Work with a fixed format.** Simple translation, basic coding, fill-in-the-form reports, calculation, summarizing long texts. Work with a clear answer that used to take a lot of hands. The menu translation above is one example, and so is the autocomplete that fills in the next line the moment a programmer types one. In this kind of work, "I do this fast and accurately" was the skill itself — and that skill is the first to become common.

**Stage 2. The expert's analysis.** This is where people start to be surprised. A doctor reading an X-ray or CT to pin down a disease, the work of forecasting demand and producing a design. Judgments only people who studied for years could make. But diagnosis and prediction, when you get down to it, are "looking at the given material and picking the right answer within a fixed framework." A fixed framework means it can be scored, and if it can be scored, it becomes work AI is strong at. The expert doesn't disappear. They just give up the seat of "the person who does this best."

**Stage 3. Reading people's minds.** This is the most unexpected part. People tend to believe that "AI may not know the data, but only a human knows the human heart." Yet recommendation algorithms guess fairly well what we'll click next. Ads find the spot where we pause, and they're getting better at predicting which content will blow up. It's not strange. AI learned, from the traces billions of people left behind, what draws them in and what bores them. The people one person meets in a lifetime number a few thousand; AI sees an incomparably larger record of the human heart. Reading people's minds, far from being the last line of defense, is closer to the kind of work AI does best, precisely because the scoring is clear.

Tie the three stages into one line and it comes out like this: if producing the answer is what makes me valuable, that value is the first to be pushed out.

## The seat that carries the work all the way through, and the seat that watches over it

Up to here it's been the story of AI producing answers. The next two stages are different. We look at what happens not just to producing answers, but to the seat that runs the work itself, and the seat where a person watches over it.

**Stage 4. Carrying many steps all the way through on its own.** The old AI was an assistant that you asked once and answered once. Now, give it a single goal and it walks through the middle steps on its own. It finds the material, organizes it, fills in the forms, processes it, and moves on to the next step. Picture the flow inside a company where a document automatically rides through the sign-off stages. The middle work people used to handle by hand shrinks, and the person is narrowed down to just deciding "what to do." In effect they're pushed up from the seat that does the work to the seat that sets the direction.

**Stage 5. The moment oversight gets in the way instead.** This is the most important turn in this piece. We usually believe that having a person check once more makes things safer. Often that's true. But when two conditions are met together, the story flips: when AI's error rate is lower than a human's, and when, on top of that, the work can be undone even if it goes wrong.

At that point a person's review stops being a safeguard and becomes noise. When a person pores over a result that's almost all correct, they fix the fine parts for no reason, slow the work down, or slip in new mistakes that weren't there. Picture a reviewer who, trying to catch typos in text that was typed flawlessly, touches a perfectly good sentence and only makes things slower. So the seat that "checks one more time" quietly disappears. The only thing that doesn't disappear is responsibility. Without ever actually looking, one person's name remains, in name only, as the one who takes responsibility if something goes wrong.

There's one caveat attached here. Stage 5 is, after all, a story limited to work that competes over "who's more accurate." In a seat that competes on accuracy, human review becomes noise. But oversight that decides not accuracy but "who takes responsibility" and "what matters more" is a different grain. That isn't something you can win on accuracy, so it lasts far longer. That story is handled separately in a later stage.

## So, on to the next part

This is the front end of the landscape of desk work, the work done with the head. The clearer the answer, the faster it falls, and at the point where human review becomes noise, the seat quietly empties out.

One natural objection remains. "Then isn't work done with the hands, the feel learned through the body, safe?" Part 2 follows that question. From repetitive factory work to manual dexterity, and on to the "intuition" that seems like it'll hold out a long time, stages 6 through 8 look at why the physical world falls more slowly — but, in the end, falls all the same.
