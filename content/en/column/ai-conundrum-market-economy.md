Ask AI to handle a document now, and it is hard to call it a toy. It writes code, cleans up tables, drafts customer support replies, and summarizes meeting notes. It still gets things wrong, but plenty of it works. AI is not a technology running on pure hype.

And that is where the conundrum begins. If AI cannot make money, it is a bubble. The investment is too large to recover, and the burden eventually falls back on the market. But if AI does make money, the problem does not disappear. If that profit comes from hiring fewer people and shrinking wage income, corporate earnings may rise while the purchasing power of the whole market weakens. If AI fails to make money, it is a problem. If it succeeds, it can still be a problem. That is why the more AI advances, the more unstable the market economy we know can become.

## The technology is real. That makes it harder

The internet was not fake during the dot-com bubble either. The problem was not the internet itself. The problem was the business models built on top of it. Traffic was high, but profits were missing. Growth was visible, but cash flow was weak. Once fresh investment dried up, the weakest companies collapsed first.

AI has a similar side. Model companies can grow revenue and still be buried under training costs, inference costs, talent costs, and data center costs. AI app startups are in an even more fragile position. If an app is just a thin layer of summarization, automation, generation, or analysis, it can be copied quickly. A smart, diligent person sitting next to you can try it once and build something similar.

But there is a clear difference from dot-com. AI actually does work. It already performs well in documents, code, design, search, analysis, and customer support. So the question now is not whether AI is real. The harder question is this: whose income does AI raise, and whose income does it cut?

## If AI does not make money, it cannot pay back the investment

If AI does not make money, the story is simple. The investment cannot be recovered. Data centers, GPUs, power, cooling, talent, and model training cost too much. If customers do not keep paying enough to justify those costs, one blunt question eventually has to be answered.

Who pays for all of this in the end?

AI app startups face the same problem. They may look as if they are growing fast. But if the feature is thin and easy to copy, they are hard to defend. Individual developers can build cheaper versions. Big tech can distribute similar features more widely. Model companies can simply make the feature part of the base product. A startup stuck between those players can find that the money it raised has become a burden.

In that case, AI becomes a burden on the market economy. The technology improves, but the investment structure around it fails to produce durable profit.

## If AI does make money by replacing people, the problem remains

But what if AI does make money? That is where the conundrum grows sharper. From the point of view of a single company, AI is a very rational tool. If AI costs less than hiring a person, and if the result is good enough, the company hires fewer people. It cuts labor costs and raises margins.

But if every company makes the same choice, the problem becomes larger. One company's labor cost reduction is that company's profit. Many companies' labor cost reductions can become a decline in society-wide income. Those wages used to flow into restaurants, rent, insurance, hospitals, tutoring, cars, and subscription services.

For a company, wages are a cost. For the economy as a whole, wages are demand.

If we miss that one line, we misunderstand the AI economy. It is not enough to say that AI raises productivity. What matters is where the gains from that productivity go. If they become lower prices, consumers benefit. If they become higher wages, workers benefit. If they become new jobs, the broader market lives. If they pile up only as returns to capital, only a small group benefits.

## A jobless boom cannot last long

At first, the stock market may like it. If companies say, "AI raised productivity, we do not need to hire as many people, and margins are improving," investors may applaud. If revenue stays the same and costs fall, profit margins look better.

But over time, another question appears. Where are the consumers?

AI can lower prices. The problem is that lower prices are not enough compensation for someone who lost income. If a person's monthly paycheck is shaken, saving a few dollars on an app subscription does not stabilize life. Rent, healthcare, education, insurance, and loan payments do not fall immediately just because AI gets better.

A jobless boom can look impressive in the numbers. But people do not live on average productivity. They live with this month's rent and next month's job postings. If wage income weakens, consumption weakens. If consumption weakens, corporate revenue starts to weaken as well. Then one question remains: margins improved, but where is the revenue growth?

## The usual answers are too slow or too weak

Some say AI will raise human productivity. That is true. But if the market does not grow, the moment three people can do the work that used to take ten, the other seven may no longer be needed.

Some say AI will create new demand. That is also true. But if AI replaces existing work faster than new markets open, the transition becomes rough.

Some say everyone can use AI and become a business owner. It sounds attractive. Reality is different. AI lowers the cost of making a product. It does not erase the cost of finding customers, earning trust, or building distribution. If everyone becomes a producer, supply explodes. That does not mean everyone can sell enough to live.

Some say the government can redistribute. The direction is right. But policy moves more slowly than technology. A company can reduce headcount within a few quarters. Taxes, welfare, education, and labor law can take years. In the meantime, people feel the anxiety first.

So for AI to help the market economy, it is not enough for the good effects to exist. Those effects must be faster and larger than the pace of displacement.

## The market has to get bigger

There is a limit to how much margin companies can squeeze out of existing businesses with AI. AI can reduce the cost of document processing, customer support, coding, and administrative work. But it cannot eliminate raw materials, electricity, land, equipment, logistics, safety, or regulation. AI reduces friction in information. It does not delete the laws of physics.

That is why companies and states eventually have to move into the physical world to find new profit. Power grids, nuclear energy, data centers, semiconductors, manufacturing automation, logistics, agriculture, healthcare, elder care, housing, defense, space, disaster response.

In these areas, AI alone is not enough. Land is needed. Equipment is needed. Permits are needed. Safety officers are needed. Even when robots arrive, people still have to install them, repair them, inspect them, and coordinate around them. The real world is always messy. Labels are crooked, sensors jump because of dust, and customers change their requirements from yesterday.

AI can reduce people inside existing markets. But when companies open new markets and solve physical bottlenecks, people are needed.

## Individuals cannot build infrastructure

An individual cannot build a power grid, a nuclear plant, a port, a space program, a data center network, or manufacturing infrastructure. Those are jobs for big tech, large corporations, and governments. The capital is too large, the regulation too heavy, and the responsibility too serious when something goes wrong.

So telling individuals that everyone should become a founder is not an answer. A business owner is not someone who merely has an AI account. A business owner is someone with customers and distribution.

What individuals can do is different. They can stand where they are needed when the larger system expands. The person who connects the field and software when AI enters physical industries. The person who connects technology and money. The person who connects equipment and people. That position is harder to erase.

## The people who become expensive

The people needed in the next phase are not people who calculate better than AI. They are people who can make AI earn money in the real world without causing damage.

They need to use AI as basic equipment. Research, design, documentation, calculation, code, simulation, automation, review. AI has to be built into their work. At the same time, they need to understand a real field. Manufacturing, healthcare, logistics, energy, construction. Some world where money and responsibility actually move.

They need fundamental learning ability. The ability to break down a new field, find the key variables, and build a structure. Abstraction and structuring belong here.

They need fundamental literacy. Not just the ability to read text. They need to read contracts, papers, code, logs, dashboards, regulations, market reports, and AI outputs, and then spot what is missing.

They need fundamental stamina. The stamina to keep learning, go to the field, meet people, and fix failed automation again. Knowledge work in the AI age is more physical than it looks.

And finally, they need to get along with people. They need to listen to people in the field instead of looking down on them. They need to translate between developers, managers, and customers. When resistance appears, they need to understand what people are afraid of. In infrastructure work, nothing moves without trust.

## The question has to become more concrete

AI raises productivity. That much is almost certain. But higher productivity does not automatically make the market economy healthier. The gains have to flow into wages, lower prices, new jobs, new markets, or broader ownership.

If they do not, AI can become a technology that lets us make more with fewer people while also reducing the number of people who can afford to buy what gets made. The technology succeeds, and the market still becomes unstable.

So the question we should ask is no longer "How smart will AI get?" The questions are more concrete now. What physical problem will AI solve? Who will keep paying for that solution? And whose income will that money become? If we cannot answer those three questions, AI can succeed and still shake the market.
