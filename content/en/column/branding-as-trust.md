A few years ago, the easiest way to guess whether someone was good at their job was to read what they wrote. A proposal, a report, an email — it told you a lot. If the sentences were clean and the logic held, you thought, ah, this person can probably do the work. Lately that signal has stopped working. Everyone's writing is smooth now. An email from a first-year and an email from a ten-year veteran come out with the same polish once a model has run over them. Clean prose no longer proves that the person behind it is sharp.

So what's left? When writing gets cheap, the thing behind the writing gets expensive. That thing is trust. Which is why I now argue the opposite of what you'd expect: in an age when AI prints first drafts for free, branding doesn't matter less — it matters more. Just not the branding you're picturing.

## Branding isn't fame, and it isn't a slick bio

When people hear 'branding,' they usually think of one of two things. One is becoming famous, having a big following. The other is dressing up your bio so it sounds impressive. Neither is the point.

Here's the most accurate definition I know. Branding is getting the market and the people around you to recall the SAME answer to one question: 'What does this person do? What problem do they solve?' That's it.

Imagine a street with ten restaurants. Nine of them blur into 'some place to eat.' But about one, everyone says the same thing: 'oh, that's the great noodle place.' That single shared sentence is the brand. Not because the owner shouts that their noodles are the best, but because the same line is lodged in many customers' heads. That's different from fame. Even if only the neighborhood knows you, within that neighborhood you have a crisp brand.

## Trust is the accumulated belief that 'hand it to them and mistakes go down'

The core of that shared answer is trust. There's no need to make trust grand. It has a very practical definition: the accumulated belief that 'if I hand this to them, fewer things go wrong.'

Anyone who has worked on a team knows the feeling. You give a task to one colleague and you relax. They catch what was missing, they say so when they don't know, and the thing you agreed on arrives on the day you agreed on. You give the same task to another colleague and you keep glancing back. You re-check their work, you patch the gaps yourself. The first person has built a trust asset. Not through eloquence, but through time — output that held up to its promise, again and again.

This asset doesn't appear all at once. It accrues by keeping small promises many times, and it collapses by breaking one big promise once. That's what makes it expensive. You can't buy it in a single stroke, with money or with words.

## Why trust gets more expensive in the AI era

Now to the heart of it: why is trust getting expensive precisely now?

Writing skill and the signal of trust used to travel together. A well-organized piece of prose was indirect evidence that the mind behind it was organized too. But the moment a model can smooth anyone's sentences, that link snaps. Good writing now tells you 'this person used a good tool' — and not much more.

Think of it like a shop sign. You used to be able to guess the craft from the sign alone, because the owner had to carve and paint it by hand. Now anyone hangs the same gleaming sign for free. The sign tells you nothing about what's actually made inside. So where do people's eyes go? Behind the sign — to what was really built, whether it checks out, whether this person's word can be trusted.

The more common sentences become, the more the difference moves to the evidence behind them and to whether you can be verified. Anyone can say something that sounds right, so what matters now is whether the saying turns out to be true. That's why the price of trust rises — not because supply shrank, but because trust is nearly the only signal left that can't be faked.

## The five parts of branding, and how they connect

Leave branding as a vague 'image' and it stays out of reach. Break it into five parts and it sharpens. And these five are linked like a chain.

First, identity: who you are. Second, problem: what you solve. Third, evidence: proof you can solve it. Fourth, repeated signal: do people remember you pointing in the same direction. Fifth, trust: do your words and outputs hold up over time.

The order matters. Identity and problem must be clear first, because they decide what counts as evidence. Evidence has to accumulate before the repeated signal carries any weight. Point the same direction long enough, and it hardens into trust. Run it backwards and it falls apart. Repeat a signal with no evidence and it's just self-promotion; scatter evidence with no identity and nothing lands as a single sentence in anyone's head.

Take a cook. Identity: 'I make home-style food.' Problem: 'I bring the quiet care of a home meal into a restaurant.' Evidence: every single bowl that goes out to a customer each day. Repeated signal: diners who all describe the place the same way. And when that taste doesn't waver for years, it becomes trust. Five parts strung onto one thread.

## Rule one: narrow and sharp on the outside, broad on the inside

Now the two rules that actually do the work. First: keep your outward position narrow and sharp, and let your inner world stay broad.

People can't remember someone 'interested in everything.' There's only one slot in the mind. When we recall someone, we store them as a single word, a sentence at most. Cram ten things into that slot and nothing survives. 'Someone who does a bit of everything' lands in the same slot as 'someone I'm not sure what they do.'

So the position you show the world has to narrow to one thing. A musician who plays several instruments still says, on stage, 'I'm a cellist.' That's how people remember her and call on her when a cello is needed. That she also plays piano, composes, and tinkers with sound backstage belongs to her inner world. That breadth makes her cello deeper. But to the outside, she's engraved in a single word.

Don't misread this. Narrowing doesn't mean shrinking. Inside, you can be as broad and deep as you like — in fact you should be. Just don't write all of it on the sign, or the sign can't be read. Wide on the inside, narrow on the sign. That's the whole of it.

## Rule two: precision over size

Second: don't speak big, speak precisely.

'I'm going to revolutionize this field' sounds grand, but with no evidence behind it, it reads as bluster. And bluster eats trust. Once an inflated claim gets caught, people start discounting even the true things you say afterward.

The opposite — small, specific, verifiable claims — builds trust. 'I solved this one problem, this way, this much' is far stronger than 'I'll change the world.' It looks smaller, but it can be checked. Ten small claims that check out are sturdier than a hundred shouts of 'I'll change the world.'

Think of running. 'I'm an elite runner' gives you nothing. But 'I run 5K in 22 minutes, consistently' is verifiable, and therefore believable. The version with a small but clear number beats the version with only grand adjectives every time. Precision is the currency of trust.

## In the end, branding is selection

Compress the five parts and two rules into one line: branding is selection. Choosing one center, and sending everything else to support it.

The person who can't choose a center tries to show everything and ends up leaving nothing. The person who chooses a center doesn't lose the rest — the rest becomes the root system that makes the one thing deeper. Narrowing isn't discarding; it's gathering.

In an age when AI makes sentences for free, smooth words no longer prove you. One thing remains: do people recall the same answer when they think of you, and does that answer hold up against time? A single line from someone with both is worth more than a thousand a model has polished. That's why trust — and branding — matters more now, not less.
