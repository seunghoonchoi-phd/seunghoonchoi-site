A few years back, spotting who was good at their job was simple. You read what they wrote. One proposal, one report, one email. If the sentences were clean and the logic clicked, you thought, "Ah, this person can do the work."

That trick doesn't work anymore. Everyone's writing comes out smooth now. An email from a brand-new hire and an email from a ten-year veteran look the same once a model has run over them. Clean writing is no longer proof of skill.

So what's left? When writing gets cheap, the thing behind the writing gets expensive. That thing is trust.

So these days I argue the reverse. The more AI cranks out free first drafts, the more branding matters, not less. Just not the branding you're probably picturing.

## What other people say about you in one sentence

When people hear "branding," they usually think of one of two things. One is getting famous, having lots of followers. The other is dressing up your bio to sound impressive. Neither is the point.

The most accurate way to put branding is this. "What does this person do? What problem do they solve?" When the market and the people around you all recall the same answer to that question, as if they'd rehearsed it together, that's branding. That's all of it.

Say there are ten restaurants on your block. Nine blur together into "just a place to eat." But about one, everyone says the same thing: "Oh, that's the great noodle place." That one line of agreement is the brand. Not because the owner yells that their noodles are the best, but because the same sentence is lodged in many customers' heads. That's not the same as being famous. Even if only the neighborhood knows you, inside that neighborhood you have a crisp brand.

## Trust is when handing it over means fewer things go wrong

The heart of that agreement is trust. No need to make trust sound grand. There's a very practical definition: the belief, stacked up over time, that "if I hand this to them, fewer things go wrong."

Anyone who has worked on a team knows it. Hand a task to one colleague and you relax. They catch what got missed, they say so when they don't know, and on the day they promised, the thing they promised shows up. Hand the same task to another colleague and you keep feeling uneasy. You re-check what you already checked, you patch the gaps yourself. The first person has built up a trust asset. Not from being smooth-talking, but from time: output that held up to its promise, again and again.

This asset doesn't show up all at once. It stacks by keeping small promises many times, and it crashes down the moment you break one big promise. That's what makes it expensive. You can't buy it in one shot, not with money and not with words.

## Now good writing just means AI cleaned it up

This is the core. Why is trust getting expensive right now, of all times?

There used to be a rough link between the signals of trust and writing skill. A well-organized piece of writing was indirect evidence that the mind behind it was organized too. But the moment a model smooths anyone's sentences, that link snaps. Good writing now tells you "this person used a good tool," and nothing more.

Think of it this way. You used to guess the craft from the shop sign alone, because someone had to carve and paint that sign by hand. Now everyone hangs the same gleaming sign for free. The sign tells you nothing about what's really being made inside. So where do people's eyes go? Behind the sign. To what was actually built, whether it holds up, whether this person's word can be trusted.

The more common sentences become, the more the difference shifts to the evidence behind the sentence and to trust. Anyone can say something that sounds right, so what matters now is whether the saying turns out to be true. That's why the price of trust goes up. Not because supply shrank, but because trust is just about the only signal left that can't be faked.

## Branding is five pieces linked into one chain

Leave branding as a vague image and you can't get a grip on it. Break it into five pieces and it sharpens. And these five are linked like a chain.

1. Identity. Who you are.
2. Problem. What problem you solve.
3. Evidence. Proof you can solve it.
4. Repeated signal. Do people remember you as someone pointing in the same direction.
5. Trust. Do your words and your output hold up over time.

The order matters. Identity and problem have to be clear first, because they decide what you'll use as evidence. Evidence has to stack up before the repeated signal carries any weight. Point the same direction long enough, and it hardens into trust. Run it backwards and it falls apart. Repeat the signal with no evidence and it's just self-promotion. Scatter evidence with no identity and you don't land as a single sentence in anyone's head.

Take a cook. Identity: "I make Korean food." Problem: "I bring the clean care of a home-cooked meal into a restaurant." Evidence: every single bowl that goes out to a customer, day after day. Repeated signal: the diners who all call the place "the clean Korean spot." And when that taste doesn't waver for years, it becomes trust. Five pieces strung onto one thread.

## Rule one: narrow the sign, widen what's inside

Now for the two rules that actually work. First, keep the spot you hang outside narrow and sharp. Keep the world you hold inside wide.

People can't remember "the person who's into everything." There's only one slot in the mind. When we recall someone, we usually store them as one word, a sentence at most. Cram ten things into that slot and nothing survives. "The person who does a bit of everything" ends up in the same slot as "the person I'm not sure what they do."

So the position you show the outside has to narrow to one thing. A musician who plays several instruments still says on stage, "I'm a cellist." That's how people remember her and think of her when a cello is needed. That she also plays piano, composes, and tinkers with sound backstage is the work of her inner world. That breadth makes her cello playing deeper. It's just that to the outside, she's engraved in a single word.

Don't get this wrong. Narrowing doesn't mean getting smaller. Inside, you can be as broad and deep as you want. In fact you should be. Just don't write all that breadth on the sign, or the sign can't be read. Wide on the inside, narrow on the sign. That's the core.

## Rule two: drop the bluster, talk in numbers

Second, don't talk big, talk precisely.

"I'm going to revolutionize this field." It sounds grand, but with no evidence behind it, it reads as bluster. And bluster eats away at trust. Once an inflated claim gets caught, people start discounting even the true things you say afterward.

The opposite, small and specific and verifiable, builds trust. "I solved this one problem, this way, this much" is far stronger than "I'll change the world." It looks small, but it can be checked. Ten small promises that check out, stacked together, are sturdier than a hundred shouts of "I'll change the world."

Think about running. "I'm a top runner" gives you no information. But "I run 5K in 22 minutes, consistently" can be checked, and so you believe it. The version with a small but clear number beats the version with only grand adjectives, every time. Precision is the currency of trust.

## Branding isn't throwing away, it's gathering

Compress the five pieces and two rules into one line and it's this. Branding is a choice. You pick one center and send everything else to the spot that supports it.

The person who can't pick a center tries to show everything and ends up leaving nothing behind. For the person who picks a center, the rest of their abilities don't vanish. They become the roots that make that one thing deeper. Narrowing isn't throwing away, it's gathering.

In an age when AI makes sentences for free, smooth words no longer prove you. One thing is left. Do people recall the same answer when they think of you. And does that answer hold up against time. A single line from someone who has both is worth more than a thousand lines a model polished. That's exactly why trust, and branding, matters more now, not less.
