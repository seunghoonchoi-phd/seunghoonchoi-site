A few years ago, telling who was good at their work was simple. You read what they wrote. One proposal, one report, one email. If the sentences were clean and the points lined up, you thought, "Ah, this person can do the work."

That method doesn't work now. Everyone's writing comes out smooth. An email from a new hire and an email from a ten-year veteran both turn out equally polished once you run a model over them. Clean writing is no longer proof of skill.

So what's left? Once anyone can easily produce smooth writing, the thing behind the writing becomes valuable. That thing is trust.

So these days I say the reverse. The more AI drafts for free, the more branding matters, not less. But not the branding we usually picture.

## What other people say about you in one sentence

When people hear branding, they usually picture one of two things. One is getting famous, having lots of followers. The other is dressing up your bio to sound impressive. Neither is the point.

The most accurate way to put branding is this. "What does that person do? What problem do they solve?" When the market and the people around you each recall the same answer to that question without ever comparing notes, that is branding. That's all of it.

Say there are ten restaurants on your block. Nine blur together into "just a place to eat." But about one, people all say the same thing. "Oh, the place that does great noodle soup." That one line of agreement is the brand. Not because the owner shouts that their noodle soup is the best, but because many customers remember the same sentence. It is different from being famous. Even if only the neighborhood knows you, inside that neighborhood you have a clear brand.

## Trust is when handing it over means fewer things go wrong

The core of that agreement is trust. There is no need to make trust sound grand. There is a very practical definition. The belief, built up over time, that "if I hand this to them, fewer things go wrong."

Anyone who has worked on a team knows this. Hand a task to one colleague and you feel at ease. They catch what got missed, they say so when they don't know, and on the day they promised, the thing they promised arrives. Hand the same task to another colleague and something keeps nagging at you. You re-check what you already checked, and you have to fill the gaps yourself. The first colleague is the one who has built up trust. Not from being a smooth talker, but from time: output that held up to its promise, every time.

This belief does not appear all at once. It builds by keeping small promises many times, and it collapses the moment you break one big promise. That is what makes it valuable. You can't get it in one shot, not with money and not with words.

## Now good writing just means AI cleaned it up

This is the core. Why is trust becoming valuable right now, of all times?

There used to be a rough connection between the signals of trust and writing skill. A well-organized piece of writing was indirect evidence that the mind behind it was organized too. But the moment a model smooths anyone's sentences, that connection breaks. Good writing now tells you only "this person used a good tool," and no more.

Think of it this way. You used to guess the craft from the shop sign alone, because someone had to carve and paint that sign by hand. Now everyone hangs the same gleaming sign for free. The sign tells you nothing about what is really being made inside. So where do people's eyes go? Behind the sign. To what was actually made, whether it holds up under checking, whether you can believe what that person says.

The more common sentences become, the more the difference moves to the evidence behind the sentence and to trust. Anyone can say something that sounds plausible, so what matters now is whether the saying turns out to be true. This is why trust becomes valuable. Not because supply shrank, but because trust is about the only signal left that can't be imitated.

## Branding is five pieces that connect in order

Leave branding as a vague image and you can't get hold of it. Break it into five pieces and it sharpens. And these five connect in order.

1. Identity. Who you are.
2. Problem. What problem you solve.
3. Evidence. Proof that you can solve it.
4. Repeated signal. Whether people remember you as someone pointing in the same direction.
5. Trust. Whether your words and your output hold up over time.

The order matters. Identity and problem have to be clear first, so that what you show as evidence is decided. Evidence has to build up before the repeated signal carries weight. Point in the same direction long enough, and over time it becomes trust. Go the other way and it falls apart. Repeat the signal with no evidence and it's just self-promotion. Scatter evidence with no identity and you don't stay in people's heads as one sentence.

Take a cook. Identity is "I make Korean food." Problem is "I bring the clean care of home cooking into a restaurant." Evidence is each bowl that goes out to a customer, day after day. The repeated signal is the diners who all call the place "the clean Korean spot." And when that taste does not waver for years, it becomes trust. Five pieces connected into one line.

## Rule one: narrow what you put outside, widen what's inside

Now for two rules that actually work. First, keep the spot you put outside narrow and sharp. Keep the world you hold inside wide.

People can't remember "the person who's into everything." There is only one slot in the mind. When we recall someone, we usually store them as one word, a sentence at most. Stuff ten things into that slot and nothing is left. "The person who does a bit of everything" goes into the same slot as "the person I'm not sure what they do."

So the position you show the outside has to narrow to one thing. A musician who plays several instruments still says on stage, "I am a cellist." That is how people remember her and think of her when a cello is needed. That she also plays piano, composes, and works on sound backstage is the business of the world she holds inside. That breadth makes her cello playing deeper. It's just that to the outside, she is known by one phrase only.

Don't misread this. Narrowing does not mean getting smaller. Inside, you can be as wide and deep as you like. In fact you should be wide. But put all that breadth outside and it can't be read what you do. Wide on the inside, narrow on what you put outside. That is the core.

## Rule two: drop the bluster, speak in numbers

Second, don't speak big, speak precisely.

"I am going to transform this field." It sounds grand, but with no evidence behind it, it reads as bluster. And bluster damages trust. Once an inflated claim gets caught, people from then on discount even the truth you tell by half.

The opposite, small and specific and verifiable claims, builds trust. "I solved this one problem, this way, this much" is far stronger than "I'll change the world." It looks small, but it can be checked. Ten small promises that check out, stacked up, are firmer than a hundred shouts of "I'll change the world."

Think about running. "I am a top runner" gives you no information. But "I run 5 kilometers in 22 minutes, consistently" can be checked, and so you believe it. The side with a small but clear number beats the side with only grand adjectives, every time. Precision is what makes trust.

## Branding isn't throwing away, it's gathering

Compress the five pieces and two rules into one sentence and it's this. Branding is a choice. You pick one center and send everything else to the spot that supports that center.

The person who can't pick a center tries to show everything and ends up leaving nothing behind. For the person who picks a center, the rest of their abilities do not vanish. They become the ground that makes that one thing deeper. Narrowing is not throwing away, it's gathering.

In an age when AI makes sentences for free, smooth words no longer prove who I am. One thing is left. Do people say the same answer when they think of me. And does that answer hold up over time. A single line from someone who has both is heavier than a thousand lines a model polished. That is exactly why trust, and branding, matters more now.
