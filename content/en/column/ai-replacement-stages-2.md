Walk into a car factory and you'll see robot arms repeating the same motion all day long. The spot where the welding sparks fly, the spot where the body of the car gets lifted and moved, the spot where the same screw goes into the same place. People used to do this work; now machines do. Nobody finds it strange.

Getting here took quite a while. Work that begins and ends in your head — translation, calculation, putting a report together — gave way long ago. You can see right on the screen whether the answer is right, and if it's wrong you just run it again, so a single attempt is nearly free. Physical work is a different story. The moment hands and feet come into it, a single attempt costs real money and real time. Stages 6 through 8 are the story this difference creates.

## Work that repeats the same motion is already done by machines

Stage 6 is repetitive physical labor. Work that runs the same motion in a fixed spot goes to robots. Assembly lines, picking and moving items in a warehouse, cleaning or hauling along a set route. When the environment stays fixed and the motion is always the same, a machine tires less than a person and is more precise.

This isn't news. Automation has been reshaping factories for decades. The difference is that people used to have to enter every coordinate and program every motion by hand. Now AI reads what it sees through a camera and adjusts its own movements. If an item sits a little crooked, it picks it up anyway. The range of "fixed environments" a machine can handle keeps widening, and with it the number of spots where human hands drop out.

## Manual skill holds out, but not because it's sacred

Stage 7 is skilled handwork and learning by doing. This is where it gets genuinely hard. It's not repeating a set motion — it's reading the situation and making fine adjustments at your fingertips, then trying something, seeing the result, and trying again based on that. This is the territory people commonly believe "a machine can't do."

Picture the search for a drug candidate in a lab. You mix substances, watch the reaction, and if the result is underwhelming you change the ratios and mix again. Robots that run this loop of trying and learning in a person's place are multiplying. The robot designs its own experiments, moves the reagents itself, reads the data that comes out, and decides the next experiment. Manual skill and trial-and-error go into the machine as a single bundle.

And yet the physical world falls more slowly than headwork. The reason is simple: a single attempt is expensive. A reagent disappears once you use it, a reaction takes time, and failed material gets thrown out. Code on a screen can be revised thousands of times a second, but a real experiment takes hours per run. People aren't holding on because manual skill is sacred. Trying and fixing is slow and costs money, so the machine just catches up that much later. As that cost gets cheaper and faster, this ground gives way in the end too.

## A veteran's gut feeling splits into two kinds

Stage 8 is the judgment you make on a situation that's not in the manual — and the "gut feeling." A variable you've never seen, a call that's hard to put into words. It's what we call the gut feeling of someone who's done it for a long time. A mechanic of thirty years pinpoints the problem just from the sound of the engine; a seasoned doctor feels something is off even when every test number reads normal. This looks like the last redoubt where human judgment holds out.

But treating "gut feeling" as one thing gets confusing. You have to split it in two.

One side is a prediction that eventually gets settled as right or wrong. This engine is about to fail, this patient has this disease, this customer will buy this item. The person can't fully explain why they feel this way either. But as time passes an answer arrives, and it gets graded — right or wrong. Anywhere there's a score is AI's home turf. A machine trained on countless cases has seen more situations than a person sees in a lifetime, and it catches even the subtle signals you can't put into words, as numbers. A veteran's intuition looked impressive because it was rare — and that rarity is now disappearing.

The other side is a seat where there's no right answer at all. Whether to bet money on this venture, whether to believe this direction is right and push it through. Here the "gut feeling" isn't prediction but conviction. If you're wrong, you lose. Money, time, reputation — what gets shaved off is yours. AI is good at guessing what people will want, but it doesn't want anything itself. The side with nothing to lose can't truly place a bet. So even after Stage 8 ends, all the "guessing gut" gets handed over, and only the "betting gut" remains.

## From ability to authority

That's where the story of ability ends. Stages 6 through 8 are all the kind that happens almost on its own the moment AI becomes able to do it. They're hard to stop. The cost of the physical world only buys a little time.

Look again at the one line left at the end: the conviction you bet on because you're the one who loses if you're wrong. This isn't a matter of ability. It's a matter of whether you sit there as someone who has something to lose. No matter how precise the machine becomes, the seat that takes the outcome onto its own shoulders raises a different kind of question. Who is responsible? Who holds the authority to decide?

And this seat isn't held by ability. It's settled by law and institutions, and by whom people choose to hand authority to. That's exactly the story the next part takes up. What stops AI in its tracks isn't that people do the job better — it's that the law protects the human seat for a while.
